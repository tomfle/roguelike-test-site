Examples of Roguelikes

Spelunky

Your humble author on this site is not one who likes to indulge in overstatement and exaggeration (or tautology for that matter), so it is with the greatest of caution when I say that when Derek Yu released Spelunky back in 2012 it represented the single greatest cultural and technological achievement in the history of our species.

Spelunky may at first glance appear to be quite a simple platformer, but when playing and re-playing through its ever changing caves, players will begin to notice how almost every element of the game interacts with every other one. For instance the player may well set off an arrow trap, but so might one of the game’s many infernal bats; for that matter it could just be a lowly rock that sets the arrow flying. The rock itself might have been set in motion by an explosion, perhaps caused by a proximity mine…which itself may have been triggered by a curious yeti. It might sound a little silly, but these small, ridiculous, brilliant elements quickly start to combine into emergent narratives; and thanks to the game’s procedural generation there are always new stories to be had on the next play through.

Downwell

Downwell is a stunning piece of minimalist game design that feels absolutely fantastic to play. The premise couldn’t get much simpler: your dog has fallen down what turns out to be an extraordinarily deep well and you need to rescue them - what more motivation could you need?

The simplicity and purity of Downwell is perhaps best illustrated by the fact that there are only 3 buttons for the whole game, and two of those are for moving left and right! The third button is where the developer has been really rather clever, combining the player’s jump and shoot actions into one; the first tap of the button sees the player jump, then subsequent taps will fire shots beneath the player while allowing them to control their fall. It is amazing how much depth the game wrings out of such a simple mechanic.

But Downwell is also a great demonstration of how well the core roguelike mechanics can work to create an amazing player experience. When first playing the game, getting through some of the lower levels of the well seems to be utterly impossible, but almost every time you play, you can feel yourself internalising its simple and satisfying ruleset.

In to the Breach

Into the Breach is a fascinating roguelike strategy and tactics game; it sees the player commanding a trio of gigantic robots as they valiantly attempt to save humanity from an army of colossal killer bugs.

Like the other games mentioned here, Into The Breach, uses its roguelike structure to encourage players to learn and engage with the game’s core systems, rather than trying to learn levels by rote.

The twist on this occasion is that the player can see what the bugs are going to do on their next turn, and plan their own moves accordingly. Initially this seems to merely allow the player to get out of the way of incoming attacks, but before long they’ll see that they can use their enemies moves to their own advantage. If there's anything more diabolically satisfying than getting two giant alien scorpions to attack one another, then frankly, I don’t want to know about it.
